How development and energy choices shape CO‚ÇÇ emissions

This project asks a deliberately blunt question: how do income, human development and energy systems jointly drive territorial CO‚ÇÇ emissions per capita across the world?

Using 1995‚Äì2021 data for 204 countries, I build an end-to-end, fully reproducible ML pipeline that links climate outcomes to development trajectories: from UNDP and World Bank indicators, through regression models, to SHAP-based explanations and an AI-assisted indicator search.

The project is designed to behave like a compact UN policy unit: global coverage, clean methodology, and outputs that non-technical decision-makers can actually use.

The problem

Governments and multilaterals are under pressure to raise human development while cutting emissions. Those agendas often get treated as separate dashboards, not one system.

Without a structured view of how income, health, education, electricity access, renewables and urbanisation interact, climate policy becomes a mix of slogans and partial evidence.

The risk: expensive decarbonisation plans that ignore social realities, or development strategies that quietly lock in high-carbon pathways for decades.

The analysis

I stitch together UNDP, World Bank and Our World in Data into a country‚Äìyear panel (1995‚Äì2021) and build a time-aware modelling pipeline: 1995‚Äì2012 for training, 2013‚Äì2021 as an out-of-sample test.

The core is a comparison between a linear regression baseline and a Random Forest model predicting log CO‚ÇÇ emissions per capita from development and energy features.

I then use SHAP to open the model up and quantify which levers actually move emissions, and by how much.

The solution

The result is a global emissions explainer: a model that doesn‚Äôt just predict which countries emit more, but why, decomposed into income, health, education, electricity access, renewables and demography.

On top, I build a small semantic search tool and indicator graph so analysts can discover relevant metrics by asking questions in plain language.

That combination ‚Äì clean data, interpretable ML and AI-assisted exploration ‚Äì is what makes this useful for both technical teams and policy leaders.

Model & Insights

The modelling setup is intentionally simple: one outcome, a transparent baseline, and one non-linear model. The objective is not to win a benchmark, but to produce policy-grade explanations.

We use the Random Forest as a non-linear lens on the development‚Äìemissions relationship, and SHAP to translate that lens into a set of actionable levers: which factors raise emissions, which help bend the curve, and where there is room for development with limited carbon penalty.

Target & feature design

  • Outcome: co2_per_capita_log = log(1 + co2_per_capita).
  • Predictors: gni_pc_log (income), mys (education), life_expectancy, under5_mortality_log, electricity_access_pct, renewable_energy_pct, urban_population_pct, population_growth_pct.
  • Time-based split: 1995‚Äì2012 train, 2013‚Äì2021 test.

Models & performance

Linear regression provides the baseline; a Random Forest captures non-linearities and interactions while remaining compatible with SHAP explanations.

Model Dataset R¬≤ MSE
LinearRegression test 0.794 0.151
RandomForest test 0.888 0.082

The Random Forest explains ~89% of the variance in log CO‚ÇÇ per capita in the test window, providing a strong but still interpretable basis for insight.

Global feature importance

SHAP and tree-based feature importance paint a consistent hierarchy:

  • gni_pc_log is the dominant driver of higher emissions per capita.
  • renewable_energy_pct exerts a strong negative effect: higher shares systematically push predicted emissions down.
  • Electricity access, urbanisation, education and life expectancy increase emissions on average ‚Äì reflecting that historical development has been carbon-intensive.
  • Under-five mortality and population growth play smaller, but non-zero, roles.

The global SHAP summary is the ‚Äúleague table‚Äù of levers ‚Äì it tells us where to look when designing policy.

Local stories: country‚Äìyear decompositions

SHAP values at the country‚Äìyear level show how the same model behaves across different development paths:

  • High-income, fully electrified countries typically have strong positive pushes from income, urban share and electricity access, partially offset by renewables.
  • Emerging economies show ‚Äújumps‚Äù in predicted emissions when electricity access and urbanisation accelerate.
  • Countries with high renewable_energy_pct get visible downward corrections even at similar income levels.

This is the level you need for questions like: ‚ÄúIf we scale renewables and electrification in this country, what happens to the emissions profile conditional on its income?‚Äù

AI tools for indicator discovery

Semantic search over indicators

Using a transformer model (all-MiniLM-L6-v2), I embed short descriptions of each indicator and expose a helper: search_indicators(query, top_k).

  • Queries like ‚Äúenergy poverty‚Äù, ‚Äúchild health‚Äù, or ‚Äúurban emissions‚Äù return the closest indicators in embedding space.
  • This mirrors how analysts actually think: they start with a question, not a column name.

It‚Äôs a small but concrete example of using modern NLP to make classical indicator work less painful.

Network graph of themes & indicators

I build a lightweight NetworkX graph where:

  • Nodes are indicators and thematic domains (e.g. ‚ÄúHuman development‚Äù).
  • Edges link indicators to domains, enabling quick visualisations and simple subgraph queries.

It‚Äôs intentionally minimal, but it shows how graphs, embeddings and tabular ML can be combined into a cohesive analysis toolkit for sustainability.
