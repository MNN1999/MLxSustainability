How development and energy choices shape CO‚ÇÇ emissions

This project asks a deliberately blunt question: how do income, human development and energy systems jointly drive territorial CO‚ÇÇ emissions per capita across the world?

Using 1995‚Äì2021 data for 204 countries, I build an end-to-end, fully reproducible ML pipeline that links climate outcomes to development trajectories: from UNDP and World Bank indicators, through regression models, to SHAP-based explanations and an AI-assisted indicator search.

The project is designed to behave like a compact UN policy unit: global coverage, clean methodology, and outputs that non-technical decision-makers can actually use.

The problem

Governments and multilaterals are under pressure to raise human development while cutting emissions. Those agendas often get treated as separate dashboards, not one system.

Without a structured view of how income, health, education, electricity access, renewables and urbanisation interact, climate policy becomes a mix of slogans and partial evidence.

The risk: expensive decarbonisation plans that ignore social realities, or development strategies that quietly lock in high-carbon pathways for decades.

The analysis

I stitch together UNDP, World Bank and Our World in Data into a country‚Äìyear panel (1995‚Äì2021) and build a time-aware modelling pipeline: 1995‚Äì2012 for training, 2013‚Äì2021 as an out-of-sample test.

The core is a comparison between a linear regression baseline and a Random Forest model predicting log CO‚ÇÇ emissions per capita from development and energy features.

I then use SHAP to open the model up and quantify which levers actually move emissions, and by how much.

The solution

The result is a global emissions explainer: a model that doesn‚Äôt just predict which countries emit more, but why, decomposed into income, health, education, electricity access, renewables and demography.

On top, I build a small semantic search tool and indicator graph so analysts can discover relevant metrics by asking questions in plain language.

That combination ‚Äì clean data, interpretable ML and AI-assisted exploration ‚Äì is what makes this useful for both technical teams and policy leaders.

Model & Insights

The modelling strategy is deliberately simple and unapologetically rigorous: one outcome, a transparent baseline, and one non-linear model. The goal is not to win a Kaggle leaderboard, but to decode a global emissions system in a way that policy and investment teams can reason about.

We start by understanding the geometry of the data (pairplots), move to model comparison (linear vs Random Forest), and then use SHAP to open the black box: global rankings of drivers and country‚Äìyear level narratives.

1. The geometry of development & emissions

What we did & what it shows

  • How we got here: once the harmonised country‚Äìyear panel is built, we restrict to a clean modelling subset and generate a pairplot_modeldata.png of log CO‚ÇÇ per capita, income, education, health and energy indicators.
  • Structure in the cloud: the pairplot highlights the strong, non-linear coupling between income and emissions, the tight relationships between development indicators (education, life expectancy, child mortality), and how energy access and renewables slot into that space.
  • Why it matters: this is the sanity check before any modelling ‚Äì if the scatter geometry already tells you that a straight line will be crude, you know a non-linear model is not a luxury but a requirement.
  • Takeaway: CO‚ÇÇ per capita lives on a structured manifold with income and development, not in isolation. Any model that pretends otherwise is doing climate numerology, not analysis.

2. Linear vs Random Forest ‚Äì who explains the world better?

From baseline to benchmark

  • How we got here: we run a time-aware split ‚Äì 1995‚Äì2012 as train, 2013‚Äì2021 as test ‚Äì then fit a linear regression baseline and a Random Forest regressor on identical features.
  • The scoreboard: the comparison table summarises test performance: the Random Forest beats the linear model decisively, reaching R¬≤ ‚âà 0.89 on log CO‚ÇÇ per capita with substantially lower MSE.
  • Interpretation: the uplift is the price of admitting reality: emissions respond to development in a non-linear way, with threshold effects and interactions that a linear model cannot capture, no matter how elegant its coefficients look in a PDF.
  • Governance angle: we now have two levers: a transparent baseline for quick communication, and a high-fidelity Random Forest whose behaviour can be made legible via SHAP.

3. SHAP beeswarm ‚Äì the global behaviour of the model

How development levers actually move emissions

  • How we got here: we compute SHAP values on the Random Forest predictions for the full test window, generating a beeswarm plot where each point is a country‚Äìyear and each row is a feature‚Äôs contribution distribution.
  • Reading the plot: the horizontal spread shows magnitude of impact, colour encodes feature value (e.g. low vs high income), and vertical density shows how often a feature meaningfully moves the prediction.
  • What it reveals:
    • gni_pc_log dominates ‚Äì higher income levels systematically push emissions up.
    • High renewable_energy_pct pulls predictions down, visibly bending the emissions curve.
    • Electricity access and urbanisation push emissions up on average, especially when combined with rising income.
  • Why it matters: this is not just ‚Äúfeature importance‚Äù as a bar chart; it‚Äôs the joint empirical fingerprint of how development and energy choices have shaped emissions in practice across almost three decades.

4. SHAP global importance ‚Äì ranking the levers

From intuition to an ordered playbook

  • How we got here: for each feature, we take the mean absolute SHAP value across all observations. That becomes the bar height in shap_summary_bar.png ‚Äì a global ranking of influence.
  • The hierarchy:
    • Income (gni_pc_log) sits at the top: no surprise, but now it‚Äôs quantified.
    • renewable_energy_pct and electricity access follow, framing the energy system choice as the key counterweight to development-driven emissions.
    • Human development indicators (education, life expectancy, child mortality) and urbanisation occupy the next tier: important, but second-order relative to income and energy mix.
  • Policy reading: this ranking is the short list of levers. It tells a minister, a development bank or a climate fund exactly where to look first if they want to bend emissions without derailing development: income trajectories, renewables, electricity access, then the rest.
  • Meta-point: by compressing three decades of cross-country data into this ordered set of drivers, the model moves the conversation from ‚Äúwe have many indicators‚Äù to ‚Äúthese are the levers that matter, in this order, given the data we actually observe.‚Äù
